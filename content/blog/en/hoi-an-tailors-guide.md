Hoi An has been making custom clothes for foreigners for decades. The town's tailoring reputation isn't marketing — it's the result of generations of craft, a critical mass of skilled artisans, and a competitive market that keeps quality high and prices honest. A well-chosen tailor in Hoi An will produce a garment that fits you better than anything you could buy off the rack at home, at a fraction of the price.

But "tailor shop" and "quality tailor" are not the same thing in Hoi An. The town has over 500 shops. Some are run by master craftspeople with decades of experience. Others are tourist traps with mediocre fabric and rushed finishing. This guide helps you find the former, avoid the latter, and get the most from the experience.

## Why Hoi An for Tailoring?

The short answer: historical accident plus critical mass. Hoi An's tailoring tradition developed through its centuries as a trading port — cloth merchants, fabric importers, and skilled artisans converged here. That concentration has never entirely dispersed. Today, the town has more tailoring workshops per square kilometer than anywhere in Southeast Asia, which creates the conditions for quality: skilled labor is abundant, apprentices can train under masters, and competition keeps standards from slipping too far.

The practical result is that a genuinely excellent custom garment — a suit that fits, a dress that drapes properly, a Vietnamese ao dai cut to your measurements — costs USD $60-200 in Hoi An versus USD $500-1,500+ at a comparable tailor in London, New York, or Sydney.

The caveat is that you need to know how to choose. The market is unregulated and the range of quality is enormous.

## Understanding the Hoi An Tailor Market

### Budget vs. Premium Shops

The tourist strip along Tran Phu Street and the lanes near the Japanese Bridge are heavily concentrated with mid-market and budget tailors targeting walk-in tourists. These shops often produce adequate work quickly, but "quickly" is the key word: 24-hour turnaround requires shortcuts in construction and finishing. Seams may be finished by machine only; lining may be glued rather than hand-sewn; fitting time is minimal.

Premium tailors — Yaly Couture, A Dong Silk, Thu Thuy Silk — operate differently. They take full measurements, schedule multiple fittings, use proper interlining in suits, and finish seams properly. The price is higher (sometimes significantly) but the result is a garment you'll actually wear for years.

### The Hotel Commission Problem

Many hotels in Hoi An have commission arrangements with tailor shops. When hotel staff recommend a specific tailor, they're often receiving 10-20% of what you spend. This doesn't automatically mean the tailor is bad — but it means the recommendation is not neutral. Cross-reference hotel recommendations with independent reviews on TripAdvisor, travel blogs, and Google.

### What "Custom" Means Here

True bespoke tailoring — where a pattern is drafted from scratch to your measurements — is available at the premium tailors. Most mid-range shops use semi-bespoke methods: existing patterns that are adjusted to your measurements. Both approaches are valid; the difference matters most for complex garments (suits, fitted dresses) and less for simple items (linen shirts, straight trousers).

## The Best Tailor Shops in Hoi An

### Yaly Couture

**Locations:** 47 Nguyen Thai Hoc Street (Showroom 1), 47 Tran Phu Street (Showroom 2), 358 Nguyen Duy Hieu Street (Showroom 3)
**Price range:** USD $45-700 depending on garment
**Speciality:** Occasion wear, complex garments, premium fabrics
**Best for:** Wedding dresses, formal suits, elaborate dresses with embellishment

Yaly is Hoi An's most internationally recognized tailor, employing over 300 artisans across its three showrooms. It's also Hoi An's most premium option — prices here are 2-3 times higher than mid-range shops. A men's custom suit runs USD $115-350; women's dresses USD $45-250; complex evening gowns USD $300-700.

The quality ceiling at Yaly is higher than anywhere else in Hoi An. The artisans handle complex garments well — structured suits with proper canvas interlining, beaded dresses, multi-layer formal wear. The fabric selection is extensive, including imported materials not available at other shops.

The caveat from recent reviews: quality control is inconsistent. When you're working with 300+ artisans across three locations, not every piece receives the same level of attention. For the highest-value garments (formal suits, wedding dresses), be present for every fitting and inspect seams, buttonholes, and finishing carefully before paying.

**Best use:** When you need a genuinely special garment — a wedding dress, a formal suit, an ao dai for a significant occasion — and price is secondary to quality.

### A Dong Silk

**Location:** 62 Tran Phu Street (and a second location)
**Price range:** USD $25-200 depending on garment
**Speciality:** Personal service, long-term client relationships, suits and dresses
**Best for:** Repeat clients, suits, traditional Vietnamese clothing

Founded in 1998, A Dong Silk was one of the first tailors in Hoi An to formally register as a business, and that longevity shows. The shop now employs around 70 people across two locations and has built a reputation for personal service: the staff speak excellent English, take detailed measurements, and keep your measurements on file for future orders (they ship internationally and have regular returning customers).

Price range sits between Yaly and the mid-market shops: a custom suit runs USD $90-150, dresses USD $40-100. The fabric selection is good — you'll choose from hundreds of options, ranging from Vietnamese silk to imported European fabrics.

A Dong's strength is reliability. You're less likely to have a horror story here than at a random Tran Phu Street tailor, and more likely to feel that the measurement process was thorough and the result genuinely fits.

**Best use:** Suits, dresses, and traditional clothing for travelers who want personal service and predictable quality.

### Thu Thuy Silk

**Location:** Flagship store in Hoi An (1,000m² space)
**Price range:** USD $30-200
**Speciality:** Premium fabric collection, silk garments, ao dai
**Best for:** Silk clothing, traditional ao dai, fabric shopping

Thu Thuy Silk has been operating since 1989 and runs Hoi An's largest tailoring facility by floor area. The 1,000-square-meter flagship store houses one of the town's most extensive fabric collections — thousands of textiles including Vietnamese silk in multiple weights and weaves, imported European wool, and various cotton blends.

The scale of the fabric selection is Thu Thuy's main differentiator. If you're coming to Hoi An with a specific garment in mind and a clear idea of the fabric you want, this is the place to start looking. Staff can advise on which fabric works for which garment type — a genuinely useful service when you're choosing between 50 shades of silk.

**Best use:** Silk garments, ao dai, and any project where fabric quality is the primary concern.

### Custom Tailory Hoi An

**Location:** Central Hoi An area
**Price range:** USD $25-150
**Speciality:** Fast turnaround (24-36 hours), hotel delivery, broad menu
**Best for:** Travelers with limited time, casual garments, repeat orders

Custom Tailory Hoi An has received strong ratings for its combination of speed (24-36 hour turnaround), convenience (hotel measurement and delivery), and a menu covering over 1,000 fabric options. It's a more modern operation than A Dong or Thu Thuy — the service is systematized, international shipping is available, and the processes are designed for travelers with limited time.

Quality is solid for casual garments: linen shirts, cotton trousers, simple dresses. For complex tailoring — structured suits, formal dresses — the 24-hour turnaround requires trade-offs. Build in time for a fitting even if the shop offers delivery without one.

**Best use:** Casual clothing, travelers with tight schedules, simple garments.

## How the Process Works

### Step 1: Choose Your Tailor

Do this research before you arrive if possible. Check TripAdvisor reviews from the past 6 months (not 2019 — turnover in staff affects quality). The negative reviews are more informative than the positives: look for patterns (rushed work, wrong measurements, poor finishing) rather than isolated complaints.

Visit 2-3 shortlisted tailors in person on your first day in Hoi An. Observe how they take measurements (are they thorough or quick?), look at the quality of finished garments on display, and ask questions. A tailor who takes time to understand what you want before taking a single measurement is more likely to produce what you're expecting.

### Step 2: Bring Reference Photos

The most common source of disappointment in Hoi An tailoring is a miscommunication about design. "I want a suit like this" followed by vague gesturing produces a suit that looks nothing like what you imagined. Bring printed or digital photos of the specific style you want:
- The lapel shape (peaked, notched, shawl)
- Button stance (one, two, three button)
- Trouser cut (slim, classic, wide)
- Any specific details (ticket pocket, contrast stitching)

For dresses: neckline style, sleeve type, hem length, silhouette. Exact reference photos remove ambiguity and give the tailor something concrete to match.

### Step 3: The Measurement Session

A proper measurement session for a suit takes 20-30 minutes and covers 15-25 measurements. For a dress, 15-20 minutes and 10-15 measurements. If a tailor takes your measurements in under 10 minutes for a complex garment, that's a red flag — they're either using standard patterns with minimal adjustment or they're very experienced (possible, but verify with the fitting).

### Step 4: Fabric Selection

Fabric choice is the largest variable in cost and quality. At each price point:
- **Vietnamese silk** — beautiful, lightweight, excellent for ao dai and summer dresses; requires careful care (hand wash, no direct iron). USD $10-25 per meter.
- **Cotton and linen** — the most practical and hardwearing; excellent for shirts, trousers, and casual dresses. USD $3-12 per meter.
- **Wool and wool blends** — the right choice for suits and tailored jackets intended to be worn in non-tropical climates. Mid-range shops typically stock 3-4 options; Yaly and Thu Thuy carry a broader range. USD $15-40 per meter.
- **Synthetic blends** — cheaper but you'll feel it in wear. Avoid for anything you intend to wear long-term.

Touch the fabric before committing. Weight matters for drape; thread count matters for durability. For suits, ask specifically whether the lining is sewn or glued — sewn is significantly better.

### Step 5: Fittings

Budget at least 2 days between ordering and your final pickup, and request at least one intermediate fitting. For a suit or complex dress, request two fittings. The first fitting (typically 24-36 hours after ordering) is when you catch major fit issues — the shoulder width is off, the waist is too wide, the hem is too long. The second fitting (usually day 3-4) is for final adjustments: buttons repositioned, sleeve length adjusted, seams let out or taken in.

What to check at fittings:
- Shoulders sit correctly (the seam is exactly at the shoulder point)
- Chest and waist fit without pulling or gaping
- Armhole depth allows movement without binding
- Hem length is correct
- Buttons and buttonholes are aligned
- Lining is clean and flat (not puckered)
- Seams are finished cleanly on the inside

Point out every issue, no matter how small. Good tailors expect this — it's how the final garment gets right.

### Step 6: Pickup and Final Inspection

Never accept a garment without trying it on first. If you spot issues, the tailor should fix them on the spot or within a few hours. Get a receipt with all details (garment description, fabric used, price paid, completion date) so there's no ambiguity if something needs to be addressed after collection.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Ordering too much.** The excitement of walking into a shop with 1,000 fabric samples leads many travelers to order 5-6 garments when 2-3 is more manageable. More orders means more fittings to schedule, more quality to monitor, and more to pack home.

**Choosing the cheapest option for complex garments.** A USD $60 linen shirt from a budget shop is probably fine. A USD $60 three-piece suit is not. The fabric and labor costs make it physically impossible to produce a quality structured suit at that price point. For simple garments (shirts, casual dresses, trousers), mid-range shops deliver good value. For structured suits and formal wear, budget up.

**Not allowing time for fittings.** Travelers who check into Hoi An for one night, order a dress on arrival, and expect to collect it 24 hours later before catching a bus are setting themselves up for disappointment. Allow 3 days minimum for quality tailoring; 4-5 days if you're ordering a suit.

**Ignoring red flags during measurement.** If the tailor rushes measurements, doesn't ask about your preferences, or seems inattentive, leave. There are 500 shops in Hoi An — you don't need to commit to the first one.

**Not getting a written receipt.** A verbal agreement about price, fabric, and delivery date becomes a verbal disagreement without documentation. Get everything in writing before you leave the deposit.

## The Ao Dai: Vietnam's Traditional Dress

The **ao dai** (pronounced "ow yai" in the south, "ow zai" in the north) is Vietnam's national dress — a fitted silk tunic worn over wide-leg trousers. Hoi An is one of the best places in the world to have one made, and a custom ao dai is one of the most enduring souvenirs from Vietnam.

Men's and women's ao dai both exist and are both available from Hoi An tailors. The women's version is more elaborate — typically high-collared with a fitted bodice and side slits — while the men's version is simpler and less common but equally striking.

Prices for a custom ao dai (fabric included) run USD $40-200 depending on silk quality, embroidery, and the shop. Simple ao dai in standard silk are available in the USD $50-80 range at mid-market shops; premium silk with embroidery at Yaly or Thu Thuy can reach USD $150-250.

Silk weight matters for ao dai: too light and the garment has no structure; too heavy and it doesn't flow. Ask for the tailor's recommendation based on how formal and how frequently you intend to wear it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long should I stay in Hoi An for tailoring?

**Minimum 3 days for serious tailoring; 4-5 days if ordering multiple garments or a suit.** Same-day and 24-hour options exist but require trade-offs in quality. Arrive, choose your tailor and garments on day 1, complete fittings on days 2-3, and collect on day 3-4. This schedule allows proper time without excessive waiting.

### Is it worth getting clothes made in Hoi An?

**Yes, if you choose the right tailor and garments.** Quality custom tailoring in Hoi An at USD $90-150 for a suit or USD $60-120 for a well-made dress represents genuine value compared to equivalent quality in Western cities. The experience also adds something beyond the garment itself — an understanding of craft and fit that changes how you think about clothes.

### What are the best items to get made in Hoi An?

**Suits, ao dai, and silk dresses** represent the highest value-for-money in Hoi An because tailoring adds the most to garments that fit precisely. **Linen shirts and casual dresses** are also good value — simple enough that they're well-executed at mid-range price points. Avoid ultra-budget versions of complex structured garments.

### Can Hoi An tailors ship my clothes internationally?

Yes — most established shops (Yaly, A Dong Silk, Thu Thuy Silk) offer international shipping. Expect USD $30-60 for standard international courier depending on destination and garment weight. Keep in mind that alterations after shipping are not possible, so the initial fitting needs to be right.

### What size information should I bring?

Bring your standard measurements if you have them: chest, waist, hips, inseam, shoulder width. Reference photos of the style you want are more important than standard sizes — tailors will take their own measurements, but knowing your proportions helps during the fitting conversation.
