A winning science fair project from Vietnam's national competition for middle and high school students is facing serious allegations of misconduct, including cheating and plagiarism, as the two student winners prepare to represent their country at the prestigious Regeneron International Science and Engineering Fair next week. The controversy, which emerged in early May 2026, centers on the advanced nature of the work and its apparent overlaps with existing published research, drawing scrutiny from local observers and scientific integrity advocates.

## Background on the Vietnam Science and Engineering Fair

The Vietnam Science and Engineering Fair (ViSEF) serves as the country's premier event for young scientists, targeting students from middle and high schools. It identifies top talent to advance to international stages like the Regeneron International Science and Engineering Fair, often regarded as the world's most competitive high school science competition. In the most recent ViSEF, held prior to May 2026, two high school students emerged as winners with their project titled “Development of multifunctional fire-resistant, heat-insulating, and antimicrobial polyurethane composite materials for application in construction and daily life.”

According to the fair's rules, participants must complete their projects within a 12-month period while balancing regular school responsibilities. The winning duo consists of one student studying mathematics and the other geography, fields that do not directly align with the project's focus on materials science and engineering. This mismatch has fueled questions about the feasibility and authenticity of the work, especially given its sophisticated scope involving polyurethane composites with properties for fire resistance, heat insulation, and antimicrobial effects aimed at construction and everyday applications.

Observers in Vietnam first raised concerns through local media, such as a report on [vietnamnet.vn](https://vietnamnet.vn/en/top-science-prize-project-faces-plagiarism-allegations-2501765.html), highlighting how the project appeared unusually advanced for high schoolers without specialized training in the relevant disciplines. The timing of the allegations is particularly acute, as the students are set to compete internationally in the coming week, placing pressure on organizers to address the claims swiftly.

## Allegations of Misconduct and Evidence

The accusations of misconduct stem from detailed comparisons by members of a Vietnamese scientific integrity group active on [Facebook](https://www.facebook.com/groups/LiemChinhKhoaHoc/). Sleuths within this community have identified multiple overlaps between the students' project poster and a research paper published in *RSC Advances* in February 2025. Notably, a graph featured in the students' poster is described as almost identical to one in the published article, suggesting potential plagiarism.

[RSC Advances](https://pubs.rsc.org/en/content/articlelanding/2025/ra/d4ra08952a) is a peer-reviewed journal from the Royal Society of Chemistry, known for disseminating cutting-edge research in chemical sciences. The similarities extend beyond the graph, with broader textual and conceptual parallels noted between the fair project and the journal article. While the exact extent of the overlaps remains under investigation, the allegations imply that the students may have incorporated unattributed elements from the professional research without proper acknowledgment or original experimentation.

These claims are not isolated; they reflect broader concerns about research integrity in educational competitions, where the pressure to produce groundbreaking results can sometimes lead to shortcuts. However, no official response from the ViSEF organizers or the students has been detailed in available reports as of May 2026. The situation underscores the challenges of verifying originality in student-led projects, especially when they tackle complex topics like advanced material composites.

## Implications for Vietnam's Science Education

This controversy arrives at a pivotal moment for Vietnam's burgeoning science education landscape. The country has increasingly emphasized STEM (science, technology, engineering, and mathematics) programs to foster innovation and prepare youth for global challenges. Events like ViSEF play a crucial role in this effort, showcasing student potential and securing spots in international forums that can elevate Vietnam's profile in scientific circles.

If substantiated, the misconduct allegations could tarnish the reputation of not only the individual project but also the oversight processes within ViSEF. It might prompt reviews of how projects are vetted for originality, potentially leading to stricter guidelines on source citation and independent verification. For the students involved, the fallout could range from disqualification to educational repercussions, though specifics remain unknown. Broader implications include heightened awareness among educators about the risks of plagiarism in competitive settings, possibly encouraging more robust training on ethical research practices.
