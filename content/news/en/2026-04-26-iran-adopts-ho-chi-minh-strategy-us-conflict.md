In a provocative analysis published on April 23, 2026, **Foreign Policy** argues that Iran is increasingly dictating the terms of its confrontation with the United States by emulating the guerrilla tactics and prolonged resistance playbook pioneered by **Ho Chi Minh** during the Vietnam War. This development unfolds against a backdrop of renewed U.S.-Iran hostilities under a second Trump administration, centered on oil shipping routes in the Strait of Hormuz and broader Middle East instability. The piece, titled "U.S.-Iran War: Tehran is Following Ho Chi Minh’s Playbook in Vietnam," highlights how Tehran's asymmetric warfare approach could prolong any conflict, much like the 20-year Vietnam struggle that eroded American resolve.

### Historical Parallels: Iran's Vietnam-Inspired Strategy

The core thesis of the **Foreign Policy** article draws direct comparisons between Iran's current military and diplomatic maneuvers and the strategies employed by North Vietnamese forces led by **Ho Chi Minh** from the 1940s through the 1970s. During the Vietnam War, Ho Chi Minh's forces relied on hit-and-run guerrilla tactics, deep infiltration of enemy lines, and a focus on wearing down public support in the opposing nation rather than seeking decisive battlefield victories. This approach, rooted in Maoist principles adapted to Vietnam's terrain, ultimately contributed to the U.S. withdrawal in 1973 after years of mounting casualties and domestic protests.

According to the analysis, Iran is applying similar principles in its proxy engagements across the Middle East and direct threats to U.S. interests. Tehran has bolstered militia groups in Iraq, Syria, and Yemen—echoing the Viet Cong's use of local insurgents—while avoiding large-scale conventional battles that would favor American technological superiority. The article points to recent incidents in the Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint for global oil transport, where Iranian speedboats and drones have harassed U.S. naval vessels without escalating to full war. This mirrors the Vietnam era's mining of Haiphong Harbor and ambushes along supply routes, designed to disrupt logistics and inflate costs over time.

The piece also notes the role of international diplomacy. Just as Ho Chi Minh leveraged support from the Soviet Union and China to sustain his campaign, Iran has deepened ties with Russia and China, securing arms and economic lifelines amid U.S. sanctions. Published amid reports of an extended U.S.-Iran cease-fire, the analysis suggests Tehran's patience is paying off, as American political divisions—exacerbated by Trump's return to office—hinder a unified response. However, the source acknowledges that while these parallels are striking, Iran's urban and desert environments differ from Vietnam's jungles, potentially limiting direct applicability.

It's important to note that the **Foreign Policy** article is interpretive, based on observable patterns rather than insider revelations. No specific new incidents or quotes from Iranian officials are cited beyond publicly reported events, and the author cautions against assuming an identical outcome given modern weaponry like precision drones and cyber capabilities unavailable in the 1960s.

### Implications for Global Stability and Oil Markets

The escalating U.S.-Iran dynamic carries ripple effects far beyond the Middle East, particularly for energy-dependent economies like Vietnam's. Vietnam, which imports a significant portion of its oil—around 70% according to widely known energy reports—relies on stable Persian Gulf shipments. Disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz, through which about 20% of the world's oil passes, could drive up global crude prices, as seen in past flare-ups like the 2019 tanker attacks.

For Vietnam, this means potential inflationary pressures on fuel and transportation costs. The country's export-driven economy, including tourism, could face headwinds if shipping rates rise. Historical precedents from the Vietnam War era show how prolonged conflicts indirectly boosted oil prices, contributing to the 1970s energy crisis that affected global trade. In today's context, with Trump administration policies emphasizing energy independence but still vulnerable to international shocks, any Iranian success in "calling the shots" might embolden other actors, prolonging uncertainty.

Travelers planning trips to Vietnam should monitor these developments, as higher oil prices often translate to elevated airfares and shipping costs for goods. While Vietnam itself remains geographically distant from the conflict, broader geopolitical tensions can influence regional alliances. Vietnam has maintained a neutral stance in U.S.-China rivalries, but closer U.S. ties under Trump could draw it into indirect pressures if Iran-Russia alignments strengthen.
