## Background on the Vietnam War Reference

The article opens by acknowledging a common trope in American political discourse since the Vietnam War ended in 1975: the tendency to label nearly any prolonged military engagement as "another Vietnam." Lowry clarifies that the Iran war does not mirror the Vietnam conflict in scale or nature. Unlike Vietnam, which involved hundreds of thousands of U.S. troops in a multi-year counterinsurgency effort focused on search-and-destroy operations, the Iran situation is described as a different kind of confrontation.

Drawing from historical analysis, Lowry points to Vietnam as a stark example of military superiority failing to compel surrender. The U.S.-backed South Vietnamese forces and their American allies possessed vast technological and numerical advantages over North Vietnamese and Viet Cong fighters. Yet, the enemy's ideological commitment and willingness to absorb heavy losses allowed them to prolong the fight until U.S. political will eroded. This "fanatical will," as Lowry terms it, enabled the North to outlast their foes, leading to the fall of Saigon in 1975.
