Japan's Prime Minister **Sanae Takaichi** met her Vietnamese counterpart, **Le Minh Hung**, in Hanoi on May 2, 2026, pledging deeper cooperation on energy and critical minerals to strengthen economic security. The leaders signed six agreements covering technology, agriculture, space, and infrastructure during the visit, amid shared concerns about China's territorial claims in the East and South China Seas and efforts to hedge against potential U.S.-driven trade disruptions.

## Background on the Diplomatic Meeting

The summit in Hanoi underscores the growing strategic partnership between Japan and Vietnam, two nations navigating complex regional dynamics. Takaichi's trip highlights Japan's intent to bolster ties in Southeast Asia, particularly with Vietnam, one of its key economic partners. According to **Al Jazeera**, the two sides identified economic security as a new priority area for bilateral cooperation. This focus comes as both countries seek to diversify supply chains and ensure stable access to resources essential for industries like electronics and renewable energy.

Japan has long been one of Vietnam's largest foreign investors, contributing significantly to its manufacturing and infrastructure sectors. However, recent data shows challenges: new Japanese investment in Vietnam dropped about 75 percent year-on-year to **$233 million** in the first quarter of 2026, even as bilateral trade increased 12.3 percent to **$13.7 billion** over the same period, based on Vietnamese government and customs figures. This dip may reflect global economic uncertainties, but the agreements aim to reverse the trend by fostering more resilient economic links.

The meeting also touched on regional stability. Hung and Takaichi reaffirmed the importance of resolving disputes in the South China Sea through peaceful means based on international law. Japan and Vietnam share apprehensions regarding China's assertive claims in the East and South China Seas, prompting them to expand security and economic collaborations as a counterbalance.

## Key Agreements and Energy Focus

At the core of the pledges is enhanced cooperation on energy and critical minerals, vital for global supply chains. Takaichi told reporters after the meeting that both nations agreed to strengthen close coordination to ensure stable supplies and reinforce these chains. Critical minerals, such as those used in batteries and semiconductors, are increasingly important amid efforts to transition to green energy and reduce reliance on volatile markets.

The six agreements signed span a broad spectrum: from infrastructure projects that could modernize Vietnam's transport networks to advancements in agriculture and space technology. While specifics on each deal remain limited in available reports, they signal a comprehensive approach to partnership. Energy cooperation, in particular, could involve joint ventures in renewable sources or exploration of Vietnam's offshore resources, though no exact projects were detailed.

This builds on existing ties, with Japan supporting Vietnam's energy diversification. Vietnam, rapidly industrializing, faces growing energy demands, and Japanese expertise in efficient technologies could play a key role. The agreements also align with broader Asian trends, where countries are seeking alternatives to traditional suppliers amid geopolitical tensions.

## Broader Implications for Regional Stability

The Hanoi summit reflects a strategic hedging strategy by Japan and Vietnam. Both nations are broadening economic and security ties to mitigate risks from U.S. trade policies and regional assertiveness by China. Japan's recent policy shifts, such as lifting bans on lethal weapons exports, indicate a move away from strict pacifism, potentially influencing future collaborations with partners like Vietnam.

For Vietnam, these ties enhance its position as a manufacturing hub, attracting more investment in high-tech sectors. The focus on critical minerals addresses supply vulnerabilities exposed by global events, ensuring smoother operations for industries that drive economic growth. Trade growth to **$13.7 billion** in the first quarter suggests underlying strength, despite the investment slowdown.

In the context of the South China Sea, the peaceful resolution commitment reinforces international norms. This could stabilize maritime routes crucial for trade, benefiting economies across Asia. Visitors to Vietnam's coastal areas, such as those near disputed waters, might notice increased naval activities, but diplomatic efforts like this aim to maintain calm.
