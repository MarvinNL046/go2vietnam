# USS Gerald R. Ford Completes Longest Deployment Since Vietnam War

The **U.S.S. Gerald R. Ford** aircraft carrier is returning to Norfolk, Virginia, on Saturday after nearly a year at sea, marking the longest deployment by a U.S. warship since the Vietnam War. What began as a routine peacetime cruise in June 2025 transformed dramatically when the ship was diverted to combat operations in the Caribbean and Middle East, straining both crew and vessel.

## From Peacetime Cruise to Combat Operations

The deployment began on June 24, 2025, as a scheduled peacetime mission with planned port calls in the Mediterranean and North Sea. The situation changed drastically in October when the Ford was docked in Split, Croatia. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth ordered the carrier to the Caribbean in preparation for a U.S. commando raid in January that resulted in the seizure of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro. Following that operation, Hegseth redirected the ship to the Middle East to support military operations against Iran.

According to [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/15/us/uss-ford-long-deployment.html), the extended mission pushed the deployment well beyond the typical timeframe. Standard warship deployments last around six months, while aircraft carrier deployments occasionally extend to eight months. The Ford's nearly twelve-month deployment represents a significant extension that has raised concerns about crew welfare and ship maintenance.

## Challenges Faced by Crew and Vessel

The extended deployment took a toll on both personnel and equipment. The approximately 4,500 sailors aboard the carrier when fully staffed experienced multiple hardships during the mission. A major fire destroyed the sleeping quarters for hundreds of sailors, creating severe crowding and discomfort. The crew also reported food shortages, delays in mail delivery, and mechanical problems with the catapult and recovery systems essential for launching and recovering warplanes from the flight deck.

These challenges highlight the strain that extended deployments place on modern naval vessels. The mechanical wear on the Ford's systems, combined with the stress on crew morale and readiness, underscores the operational costs of maintaining extended military presence across multiple theaters.

## What This Means for Naval Operations

The Ford's return marks a significant moment for the U.S. Navy's operational tempo. Extended deployments of this length can impact the Navy's ability to maintain regular rotation schedules and crew readiness standards. The ship's experience demonstrates the challenges of rapidly pivoting from peacetime operations to sustained combat support across distant regions.

The deployment also reflects broader geopolitical tensions, particularly the U.S. military's involvement in Venezuela and Iran during 2025-2026. The ability to redirect major assets like aircraft carriers shows the flexibility of naval power projection, but the human and mechanical costs are substantial.

## Looking Ahead

As the **U.S.S. Gerald R. Ford** returns to its home port, the Navy will likely conduct thorough assessments of both the crew's condition and the ship's mechanical systems. The extended deployment provides valuable data about the limits of current carrier operations and may influence future decisions about deployment schedules and the rotation of naval assets.

The crew's homecoming after nearly a year at sea represents both a relief and an opportunity for the Navy to address the operational lessons learned from this historic deployment—the longest since the Vietnam War era.
