A Vietnam War veteran and his daughter born with a severe birth defect have filed a lawsuit against the U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs (VA), challenging the agency's denial of disability benefits tied to the veteran's exposure to the toxic herbicide **Agent Orange**. **Ronald Christoforo**, a former Army telecommunications technician who served in Vietnam, and his daughter **Michele Christoforo**, who has achondroplasia causing dwarfism, initiated the suit on Monday, April 27, 2026, in the U.S. District Court for the District of Connecticut. They argue that the VA's policy discriminates based on the veteran's gender, providing benefits for children of female Vietnam veterans with certain defects but not for those of male veterans, except in cases of spina bifida.

## Background on Agent Orange and VA Benefits

The use of **Agent Orange** during the Vietnam War, from 1961 to 1971, involved the U.S. military spraying millions of gallons of the herbicide to defoliate jungles and deny cover to enemy forces. This chemical, containing the highly toxic contaminant dioxin, has been linked to numerous health issues for veterans, including cancers, neurological disorders, and reproductive problems leading to birth defects in their children. According to the lawsuit details reported by [Military Times](https://www.militarytimes.com/veterans/2026/04/27/vietnam-veteran-daughter-sue-va-over-agent-orange-birth-defect-benefits/), **Ronald Christoforo** was exposed to Agent Orange while serving in South Vietnam.

In 1996, the VA began providing disability benefits to children of Vietnam veterans born with spina bifida, recognizing the condition's connection to paternal exposure. This was expanded in 2000 to include 18 specific birth defects for children of female Vietnam veterans, such as achondroplasia—the genetic disorder affecting **Michele Christoforo**, who applied for benefits in 2022. The VA denied her claim, stating that eligibility required the mother to have served in Vietnam or Korea. The suit contends this creates unconstitutional sex discrimination, as the same condition qualifies if the parent is female but not male, despite evidence of paternal exposure risks.

The Christoforos, represented by Yale Law School’s Veterans Legal Services Clinic, highlight stark disparities: approximately 200 children of female Vietnam veterans were born with covered defects, compared to an estimated 350,000 children of male veterans with birth defects potentially linked to Agent Orange. **Michele Christoforo** stated in a release that the VA did not dispute her condition's reality or its tie to Agent Orange but rejected the claim solely because her father served, not her mother. This case underscores the long-term human cost of the war, now over 50 years later, as affected families seek recognition and support.

## The Lawsuit's Core Arguments

The plaintiffs seek a court declaration that the VA's sex-based distinctions in benefits are unconstitutional under the Equal Protection Clause of the Fifth Amendment. They argue that if **Ronald Christoforo** were female, his child would automatically qualify for medical care, educational assistance, and monthly income support under existing laws like 38 U.S.C. § 1821. The suit emphasizes that scientific research does not justify differentiating between maternal and paternal exposure effects on offspring, a point echoed by experts.

**Linda Schwartz**, a Vietnam veteran and adviser to Vietnam Veterans of America, supports the lawsuit, noting that veterans returned with invisible wounds passed to their children. She stated that research fails to support the maternal-paternal divide. Similarly, Sen. **Richard Blumenthal** (D-Conn.) has backed related legislation to boost research on birth defects from environmental exposures like Agent Orange and burn pits in later conflicts. On Monday, Blumenthal called the denial "unjust and cruel," affirming **Michele**'s condition, her father's service, and Agent Orange's documented harm.

This legal challenge arrives amid growing awareness of intergenerational impacts from military toxins. While the VA has compensated over 4 million claims related to Agent Orange for veterans themselves, benefits for children remain limited, affecting thousands. The suit does not speculate on dioxin's exact transmission mechanisms—whether through sperm, environmental contamination post-service, or other vectors—but relies on established links to push for equitable policy.
