# Vietnam Oil Firm Urges US Navy to Clear Tanker in Hormuz Blockade

HANOI — Vietnam's state-owned oil company has appealed to the US Navy to allow one of its tankers to navigate through a blockade in the Strait of Hormuz, as revealed in a recently disclosed document. The request, dated around May 2026, underscores growing concerns over disruptions in vital global oil shipping lanes amid heightened geopolitical tensions in the Middle East. While specific details on the tanker's cargo or exact timing remain limited, the move highlights Vietnam's vulnerability to international energy supply chains.

The Strait of Hormuz, a narrow waterway between Iran and Oman, serves as a critical chokepoint for approximately 20% of the world's oil trade, making any blockade a potential flashpoint for global energy prices. Vietnam, as an import-dependent nation for refined petroleum products, relies heavily on stable access to these routes to fuel its growing economy and industrial sectors.

## Background on the Appeal and Regional Energy Dynamics

Vietnam's state oil company, widely known as PetroVietnam, plays a pivotal role in the country's energy sector. Established in 1977, PetroVietnam oversees exploration, production, and imports of oil and gas, contributing significantly to Vietnam's GDP. The company's operations extend beyond domestic fields in the South China Sea to international sourcing, including from Middle Eastern suppliers.

According to the document cited by [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/vietnams-state-oil-company-urges-us-navy-allow-tanker-through-hormuz-document-2026-05-12/), the appeal was made directly to the US Navy, which maintains a substantial presence in the region through its Fifth Fleet based in Bahrain. The US Navy often escorts commercial vessels in sensitive areas to ensure freedom of navigation, a principle rooted in international maritime law under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea.

The blockade in question appears tied to ongoing frictions in the Persian Gulf, though exact causes—such as potential escalations involving Iran or regional alliances—are not detailed in available information. Historically, the Strait of Hormuz has seen tensions, including during the 1980s Tanker War and more recent incidents involving seizures by Iranian forces. In 2019, for instance, several tankers were attacked or detained, leading to temporary spikes in global oil prices.

For Vietnam, this incident is particularly noteworthy given its strategic pivot toward energy security. The country imports over 80% of its oil needs, with key suppliers including Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Kuwait—nations whose exports predominantly pass through Hormuz. Disruptions here could exacerbate Vietnam's existing challenges, such as fluctuating crude prices and domestic refining capacity constraints at facilities like the Dung Quat refinery.

PetroVietnam's involvement in international shipping is not new; the company operates a fleet of tankers for both domestic distribution and global trade. However, direct appeals to foreign militaries are rare and signal the severity of the situation. No public response from the US Navy has been reported, and details on the tanker's destination or load—possibly bound for Vietnamese ports like Vung Tau or Ho Chi Minh City—remain undisclosed.

## Implications for Vietnam's Economy and Global Trade

This development comes at a time when Vietnam's economy is rebounding from global supply chain issues, with GDP growth projected around 6-7% for 2026 by international forecasts. The energy sector, accounting for about 10% of exports through petrochemicals and related industries, could face immediate ripple effects. A prolonged blockade might drive up shipping insurance premiums and freight costs, indirectly affecting manufacturing hubs in northern provinces like Bac Ninh and southern ones like Binh Duong.

On a broader scale, the event illustrates the interconnectedness of Southeast Asian economies with Middle Eastern energy flows. ASEAN nations, including Vietnam, have been diversifying suppliers toward Russia and the US to mitigate risks, but Hormuz remains indispensable. Speculation about broader US involvement exists, but without further documents, it's unclear if this appeal will lead to coordinated multinational efforts, similar to past operations like the International Maritime Security Construct.

Environmentally, any escalation poses risks to marine ecosystems in the Gulf, which could have long-term effects on global fisheries and biodiversity—areas of indirect concern for Vietnam's seafood export industry.

While the blockade is thousands of miles away, potential surges in global oil prices could translate to higher fuel costs, affecting domestic transportation. Expect possible increases in flight fares to major airports like Tan Son Nhat in Ho Chi Minh City or Noi Bai in Hanoi, as well as rideshare and taxi prices within the country.

Tourism, a key pillar of Vietnam's economy contributing over 10% to GDP pre-pandemic, relies on affordable mobility. Safety remains paramount; no direct threats to Vietnam have emerged from this incident. Overall, Vietnam's tourism infrastructure is resilient, and this event is unlikely to deter visits unless global tensions escalate dramatically.

## What's Next: Monitoring Developments

As of May 14, 2026, the situation remains fluid. PetroVietnam may release an official statement, and diplomatic channels between Vietnam, the US, and Gulf states could yield updates. International bodies like the International Energy Agency might convene to assess supply risks, potentially activating emergency reserves.

Travelers and businesses are advised to stay informed through reliable sources. If the tanker is cleared, it could signal de-escalation; otherwise, contingency planning for higher energy costs is prudent. Vietnam's government, through the Ministry of Industry and Trade, is likely coordinating responses to safeguard imports.

In the long term, this underscores the need for Vietnam to accelerate renewable energy transitions, such as solar and wind projects in the central highlands, to reduce reliance on imported fossil fuels. For now, the focus is on resolving the immediate impasse in Hormuz.
